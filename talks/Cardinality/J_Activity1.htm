Activity 1: Computing the Even Numbers in Two Ways in order to answer the question:

 Are the natural numbers more than the even ones? 

Warming up: 

In Sofia:

Teddy and Mitty (simjultaniouslyJ) No, they are equal!

Jenny: What if someone tells you that the even numbers are fewer since they are part of the natural numbers.

Teddy: I’ll beat him up! …

Mitty: No, in principle the two sequences are equally long but the second is twice bigger.... I said “twice bigger” since each term of the second is twice bigger than the corresponding natural number. At the same time I could say that the sequence of the even numbers is as long as the sequence of the natural ones since there is no end - after each pair of odd-even numbers there follows another pair of odd-even numbers and after each even number there is another even number – both sets are infinite.

Jenny: Are you saying that if two things are infinite they have the same number of elements? Isn’t it possible for one of the infinities to be of a greater power?

Teddy and Mitty: Hm-m-m…

Yana: If we denote the natural numbers by X then X=infinity. Let us denote the even numbers by Y. Then Y=X/2 , i.e. Y =infinity /2, which is infinity. So it depends on the point of view.

Ivan: If someone tries to tell me that the even numbers are fewer than the natural numbers I would not be able to convince him that he is not right but neither could he convince me that he is right .

Jenny: Why not? If he claims that the even numbers are fewer you could offer him a game – he names a number of his sequence and you name a corresponding number of your sequence (twice as big). And he would not be able to think of a number without a partner from your sequence. But let’s demonstrate this correspondence with robots…

Note1: The students are with a good mathematical background for that age since they have extracurricular math activities, so they "know" that the even numbers are as many as the natural ones. But their explanation about this reads: "both sets are infinite". They seem to be comfortable with the even numbers being "twice bigger" and "equally long" with respect to the natural but they become aware that every such term should be strictly defined.

Children train these robots:

  1. Add 1 to generate natural numbers
  2. Doubler to double the incoming numbers
  3. Split to split incoming numbers into two streams

Two month earlier (5 November) Ivan had constructed Doubler  as a single robot by copying the input and adding to it. We discussed then that in fact a+a= 2a. Now he constructs Doubler by using ADD1 and multiplies the input by 2. So does Yana. She adds that in order to get the odd numbers we could edit  this robot by subtracting 1. After a hot discussion how to train the Split robot the kids greatly evaluate the idea in the instructions.The students realise that the nest of the even numbers in the Split robot would contain the same numbers as the nest of the Doubler. Another thing we discuss  is the variety of ways we could get the same sequence of numbers and the importance of naming properly the cells, the birds, the robots. 

In  Plovdiv the students start with a slight generalization - they want to generate all the odd numbers greater than an arbitrarily chosen negative number. Discussion follows involving how to check if the starting number is odd or even. Finally they reach a consensus to start with an odd number and add 2 consecutively. To get the odd numbers smaller than the initial one they construct a new robot, subtracting 2 instead. (Since Christian thinks that training a new robot is boring and states that in Pascal he would have "only to change the sign", Sofia group challenges him to generate either a growing or a decreasing sequence with a single robot.) Christian suggests a new idea - to use the ADD1 and skip every second number. The rest like it but can't figure out how to implement it.

Note2: We found very productive for the kids to try out their own ideas of constructing a robot for a concrete task. The instructions were given mainly to show a different approach (when needed). Previous sessions had shown that understanding what a ready made robot does was more difficult (even with relatively simple robots like ADD1 and ADD_UP, e.g. in the case of the Factorial task).
