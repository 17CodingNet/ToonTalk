5. Are there infinite sequences that have so many terms they can't be counted?

 

We have argued about what a sequence actually is and have come to the conclusion that it depends on what you mean by sequence. A sequence could be a list of number starting in a certain place, changing according to a certain rule each time. Or it could just be the rule, e.g. n+1. 
 

Yes, you can't count to the end, we agreed that there is no end, we are still not sure if there is a beginning.

S (13-year old girl), T (13-year old boy), and A (13-year old boy) quickly decided that sequences without an end couldn’t be counted. Though there was some discussion of what countable meant. Then A as the scribe wrote sequences without a beginning or end couldn’t be counted. This led to a very long discussion of what is a sequence whether it is described as “n+1” (and hence could be started anywhere but the “full sequence” could start anywhere – A’s position) or as “+1” where you start with some number and repeatedly perform actions to obtain the next term. A even claimed that a sequence could have no beginning but an end. Everyone thought that was wrong and he backed off.

A year earlier four children 10 and 11 years old discussed this same topic and most thought that there were uncountable sequences until one of the boys suggested that there can't be since a robot could count the numbers in a sequence.
